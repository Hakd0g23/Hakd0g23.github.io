Trade-Bot

Write-up only

An automated trading system for MetaTrader 5, covering two independent strategies and a small local ops layer to run them unattended. Started as two browser-based tools, later ported to standalone Python services that share a common broker bridge, position-sizing, and alerting layer.

What it does

  • Crypto scanner — a trend-following strategy across seven major coins (vs USDT). A weekly EMA bias sets the only allowed trade direction, confirmed on the daily timeframe, with entries on a 4H pullback to a moving average or VWAP, an ATR-based stop, and a two-stage take-profit (partial at 1:1, remainder at 2:1). Market data comes from a free public API, so the scanner runs even without a live broker connection.
  • Forex/gold scanner — a completely different approach: a multi-timeframe liquidity-sweep reversal (stop-hunt) strategy across six major pairs plus gold, with no inherent trend bias. It watches for price to wick beyond a recent 4-hour high/low and snap back inside it, then enters in the reversal direction on the 15-minute chart.
  • Monitor dashboard — a local status page showing whether the broker bridge and both scanners are actually alive (a real heartbeat, not just "process running"), one-click start controls, and a full post-reboot bootstrap sequence. Registered as a background service so it survives restarts.

Risk and execution safety

Trades are sized as a fixed percentage of account risk per trade, computed from the broker's real contract specs rather than a fixed lookup table. An early version of the sizing logic instead reused the scanner's spot-market quantity formula directly as a broker lot count — fine for symbols near a 1:1 lot multiple, but on symbols with much larger contract multipliers that silently meant positions many times the intended risk. That bug, combined with a duplicate order-send issue, produced a batch of live trades with an 8.3% win rate and outsized losses before either was caught. Both are fixed now: lot size is derived from the broker's live tick value/size before every order, one open position per symbol is enforced, and stop/target direction is validated before anything is sent. Manual per-trade confirmation is also the default — nothing fires automatically unless that's explicitly turned off.

Does the edge hold up?

Both strategies were backtested bar-by-bar against real historical data (two years of history for crypto, roughly 60 days of intraday data for forex/gold) using the exact same strategy code that runs live, not a separate re-implementation, so these numbers can't drift from what's actually deployed. Results below are in R-multiples — multiples of risk per trade — rather than dollar figures, since R is independent of account size:

  • Crypto, original parameters: +5.98R over 51 trades, 56.9% win rate, 2 of 7 symbols net negative.
  • Crypto, refined parameters (current): +55.43R over 235 trades, 56.6% win rate, all 7 symbols net positive.
  • Forex/gold, without a trend filter: -27.34R over 323 trades, 30.7% win rate — a genuine negative edge over this window, not just an unlucky run.
  • Forex/gold, with a 1H trend gate and a minimum stop-distance filter (current): +5.00R over 37 trades, 37.8% win rate.

The forex trend gate exists because of a real live losing streak shortly after launch: the strategy dropped its first six closed trades in a row, including three consecutive long GBPUSD signals fired within a few hours of each other at successively worse prices — it kept calling for a reversal into a trend that hadn't actually turned. Adding a same-timeframe trend filter, and later a minimum stop-distance guard, turned a backtested negative edge into a small, non-uniform positive one — treated as a validated improvement rather than a guarantee, and not walk-forward tested.
